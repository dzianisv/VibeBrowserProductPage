Two years ago, Weber Wong was a VC at Menlo Ventures who says he wouldn’t have backed himself as a founder. Wong says he felt he lacked a burning mission, a one-of-one insight. So he quit, moved to New York, worked at a coffee shop, and enrolled at NYU’s Interactive Telecommunications Program to make art with technology. What started as a quarter-life crisis became a thesis: Silicon Valley has completely misunderstood what creative professionals actually need from AI.

That thesis has led to a $42 million Series A raise for FLORA, the creative tool company Wong founded after building real-time AI art installations that went viral in the Brooklyn tech scene. FLORA is an interface layer: a visual, node-based canvas where the world’s best creative teams can connect generative models into repeatable workflows that actually give them control. Since Wong’s time in a16z speedrun (SR004), Flora has taken off in a major way: Pentagram uses it to explore hundreds of logo variations at once. Nike listed “mastery of FLORA” as a job requirement for a new design role. Lionsgate is generating entire concept movies inside the platform.

In this conversation, Wong sits down with Jonathan Lai, General Partner at Andreessen Horowitz. The two share a fascination with real-time AI and creative tools, and their discussion cuts to the heart of a question the tech industry keeps getting wrong: what do professional creatives actually want from this technology?

Jon Lai: Weber, congratulations on raising a Series A from some fantastic investors. I definitely want to dig into what you plan to do with all that money. But first, let’s rewind and start with the origin story. You have a really fascinating and unique narrative arc. You started in investment banking at Evercore, went to Menlo Ventures, then went to NYU ITP where you ended up founding FLORA. Walk us through the moment you decided to leave Menlo—it sounds like that was a key inflection point. What were you feeling and what led you to ITP?

Weber Wong: My decision to leave was pretty impulsive. I think it was a quarter-life crisis type of thing. I really loved it there and I learned a lot and found venture very fun, but I realized three things. One, none of the companies I had seen were ones I would have wanted to start myself. They could be stacking ARR, but it wasn’t the mission I was personally interested in. Second, I wouldn’t have backed myself at the time. I didn’t feel like I was a one-of-one founder or had a unique worldview. And third, I really missed art and wanted to pursue that.

So really without much of a plan, I decided to quit my job, move to New York, work at a coffee shop, and explore the conceptual art world. The week before I left, we looked at investing in RunwayML, the text-to-video company, and that’s how I found out about NYU ITP because the founders came out of there. I did some research and learned it was a place where people use technology to make art. That really blew my mind because from my time in venture, I thought you could only use technology to make money.

When I got to New York, I got into the Brooklyn art and tech scene and met the head of ITP. We really hit it off, and he gave me a full scholarship and let me join right away. Once there, I started learning all different types of technology to make the art I wanted—holograms, real-time AI installations, shader code, physical computing, the whole works. It was really fun.

Actually Jon, I remember you wrote that piece about real-time video generation with AI and frames per second. I was like, why is this VC writing about this stuff that I’m exploring in the art world? That’s crazy. Glad we share that same interest.

Jon Lai: Yeah that’s how we met! I’m glad you reached out

Weber Wong: I was working on art projects with real-time AI mirrors. I live-streamed my life for two weeks with a GoPro on my head and streamed it to a landing page where you could see my world textured in real-time AI. Those projects started getting a lot of traction in the art and tech world, so I built a tool for others to do it as well. It was a node-based tool—webcam to real-time AI output—so you could use this powerful model on the web. People started using it a lot, it started costing me money, and I realized I should do something about this.

The initial idea was to build a fully autonomous agency where we have a fully autonomous creative stack, get some of the best creators in the world, and they do the work of a hundred people. Then I realized I don’t know anything about starting an agency, but I do love building creative tools. So why not build the world’s most powerful creative tool? Sell it to every other creative team. Scale like a venture-backed startup. Use those resources to make the tool more and more powerful to enable more creators to make great work.

That’s when we decided to start FLORA. Most importantly, I realized I just love building creative tools and helping creators make great creative work—it’s a really great job. And I would back myself for this. I was doing this for fun and building the tool initially for myself.

I also think Silicon Valley doesn’t quite understand the professional creative industry. Most of Silicon Valley thinks AI is meant for fun pictures of your dog—novelty, convenience. But there are millions of creative professionals that need to do great creative work for a living, and they’re completely getting left behind. No one is building for them. When we started, we went to work with Pentagram, the world’s best design agency, and a lot of the best teams in the world. A year since we launched, we’ve been fortunate enough to work with hundreds of world-class teams ranging from Lionsgate to Netflix to MSCHF to Nike. That’s the origin story.

Jon Lai: That’s fascinating. I love two things in particular you said. First, when you left Menlo, you realized you wouldn’t back yourself as a founder - and you had to change that. You took your destiny into your own hands, which is incredibly inspiring. Second, you started FLORA to create a product you wanted for yourself as a creative user. I think it’s one of the most powerful reasons to start a company - you solve a problem for yourself and realize many other people have a similar problem. And now you’re bringing that product to the world as a fellow creative. That’s really powerful.

I want to double-click on something you mentioned - Silicon Valley not understanding the creative industry. Let’s unpack that a bit - what are some common misconceptions Silicon Valley has about what creatives need?

Weber Wong: To frame it, imagine getting a painter to make an IDE. They’d be like, “Where do I even start?” You’d end up with something like vibe coding maybe. Now in reverse, if you get programmers and AI researchers to make a professional creative tool, they don’t even know what the roles are on a creative team—creative director, copywriter, strategist. They don’t know the creative process: first you have an idea, explore different concepts, decide what you want, refine and produce it, then hand it off.

Fundamentally, they see creative problems as something you can one-shot. I talked to a model company CEO who said, “I think movies are a problem I can solve.” But the point of movies is not to solve problems—it’s to express and have control over every shot, the camera motion, to be really creative about every single scene. Creative work isn’t necessarily required—people do it because they want to and they want control over it.

When the personal computer came out, Microsoft built an operating system on top of it. Then Adobe built a creative interface on top of that graphical user interface. A generative model is a computer—it takes an input and produces an output. You need an interface on top of that. WebGL is not a creative tool. Figma is a creative tool.

I think a lot of model companies think what they’ve built are creative tools because they don’t come from creative backgrounds. They don’t realize that what creatives really need is step-by-step creative control to get exactly what they want—not a prompt in and then some pretty pixels out that they have no control over. There are many layers above the computer to be built.

Jon Lai: If you’re approaching it from an engineer’s perspective, you see a movie or a game and think, “It’s a series of pixels that can be generated by code—a knowable problem I can solve.” But generating pixels is just the tip of the iceberg. There’s a whole team, a whole craft, a whole process behind telling a compelling story.

One thing we’ve heard from creatives over the past year is that they’re moving beyond the novelty phase of generating media. It’s no longer cool just to generate a cat video. What they’re looking for is control—how do you integrate what you’ve generated into a technical and creative workflow that makes sense, that’s compatible with other tools, in a repeatable and scalable fashion. Would you agree? And how does FLORA tackle this?

Weber Wong: That’s spot on. Creatives want more control and need it to be part of a repeatable, scalable system. If you think about the creative process—I have an idea, I explore many concepts, I decide which one I want, I refine and produce it at high fidelity, then I distribute it—AI is really good at the first portion: ideation and exploration. But it doesn’t matter for a creative unless you get exactly what you want and can hand it off.

Right now they still have to go into traditional tools to finish some things, even if 80% of the work for AI-forward teams is being done completely in FLORA. We’re adding additional control features coming out soon—image editing, video editing—where they can finish everything in FLORA.

The difference between the last creative tool paradigm, which was about making one piece of media at a time, versus FLORA is: we have all these different models you can connect together. Each model is a step of the creative process. So we can do the entire creative workflow. And because it’s modular, you can build a creative workflow bespoke to exactly how your team operates. The workflow we built for Nike is different than the one for Mac Cosmetics or Pentagram. But the universality is that all digital media is text, pixels, and moving pixels—so they’re all achievable if you know what you’re doing.

The second portion is making it a repeatable, scalable system. There are two modes of creation: manual and autonomous. Manual creation is moving the process along step by step. We’re seeing people build what we call “creative systems” where every step is connected into a workflow—they feed an input and it runs autonomously.

One of our customers is an AI-forward agency offering a new type of creative service called a “Generative Brand Sprint.” He does an entire rebrand in two weeks versus six months, completely outcompeting his competitors at the same level of quality. Typically the brand identity process takes weeks of research and a lot of manual work. He has a fully autonomous FLORA workflow that codifies his process—he takes notes from the customer call, runs it through, and gets back to them in about an hour.

Jon Lai: That’s amazing. That would have taken a team weeks or months before.

Weber Wong: We believe these creative systems are how many of the best teams of the future will work. They only produce really great work if paired with high craft—it doesn’t replace actually knowing what’s good. You have to codify that into every single step and evaluate the results. But it’s very clearly the future. The customers who are AI-forward enough to understand workflows in FLORA are executing at a completely different level.

Jon Lai: Incredible. Who are the users of FLORA today? You mentioned Pentagram and Nike. What are the typical use cases and who within these companies use FLORA?

Weber Wong: It’s very broad-based. In fashion, CPG, and beauty, people are doing e-commerce—Mac Cosmetics uses it for different products and visualizations. Nike recently posted a job description for a Generative AI Design Expert where mastery of FLORA was one of the job requirements. We reached out and started helping them with a sketch-to-360-turntable shoe workflow. We can help them go from sketch to fully rendered shoe to branded marketing campaign all in one workflow. Typically that’s three different teams.

The design director said something interesting: too often shoe ideas win just because someone’s better at Photoshop. He wants to equalize the playing field and likes that FLORA can help a shoe designer see it all the way through.

There are many in-house brand and marketing teams—from really big ones like Procter & Gamble down to smaller ones like Hebbia. We just did a case study with them—a single designer there did their entire rebrand without an agency, all in FLORA.

A lot of agencies use FLORA too. Pentagram uses it to explore hundreds of logo variations and typefaces at once, choose one, and build from there, instead of doing that by hand. People doing creative campaigns explore the craziest possible ideas extremely quickly because it’s a lot easier in AI.

There are also film and VFX use cases—someone making a music video and creating cool effects, or Lionsgate generating entire concept movies in FLORA.

And there’s a long tail of freelancers and small businesses. My founder friends use it to generate their own ads. I talked to a guy who owns a tire shop in Montana who uses FLORA for all his brand and marketing. He’s having a great time.

Jon Lai: So a lot of creative product exploration - prototyping, storyboarding, ideation. Plus marketing use-cases like branding, website redesigns, ad visualizations. A very broad set of use cases. It sounds like you might have some new product releases coming up too. What might those unlock?

Weber Wong: We keep our roadmap pretty tight—we know what we want to build for the next two years and keep it under wraps until we release it. But I’ll say we’re working on deeper control—professional-grade control like you’d see in Figma or Adobe. A better visual programming experience to build more powerful workflows, bulk generation, things like that.

We’re also working on a feature we think will be as transformational as the core product itself—it takes generative workflows and makes them easily usable by anyone. We think that will greatly increase the universe of people who can use FLORA. We also have agentic features, which we view as part two of FLORA.

Jon Lai: Let’s shift to talk about company building and the team. What’s it like to work at FLORA? Who’s on the team and what’s the culture?

Weber Wong: Our mission is to make creators more powerful. We’re an applied AI company focused on building the world’s most powerful creative tools and figuring out the right creative interface for the generative computing paradigm—both in terms of interface and product, but also from a cultural and moral perspective.

There’s a lot of anxiety in the creative world about AI. We want to be a positive force, a center of excellence for great creative work—proving that being an AI company and caring about creativity are not opposing positions.

Our culture is very high performance—I hold people to a very high bar—but we’re also extremely creative. A lot of us come from creative technology backgrounds and use a lot of creative tools. Everyone joins because they really want to build a great creative tool for creatives. It’s clearly morally positive to help people make creative stuff and express themselves—that’s a great motivation to lock in and build something great.

Over the past couple months I’ve built out a full executive team. Our CTO, Charlton, was formerly leading engineering for the core product at Frame.io, which sold to Adobe for $1.2 billion. He spent some time there, then started his own company. We met, fell in love with building together, and he joined as CTO—now he’s going to take down his former employer Adobe.

Harish, our Head of Product, founded and was CEO of a design education business called DesignLab that he scaled to $50 million in revenue. His customers started asking him to teach FLORA, he fell in love with the product, and joined us.

Carson, our Head of Ops, was leading operations for a 750-person team at Instacart Manhattan. He started a creative tech events business and an agency, fell in love with FLORA, and joined us.

We just brought in Cody, the former VP of Sales at Canva, to be our VP of Sales. And I hired Alec from Scale AI, who was leading teams building enterprise agents there. He’s focused on our agentic features and making FLORA a truly intelligent creative tool.

We’ve brought in a great team and we’re going to accelerate from here. Given customer demand and how much is left to build, we’ll be scaling up quite a bit on engineering and GTM throughout this year.

Jon Lai: What’s the one thing you look for in a potential hire? Is there a common thread?

Weber Wong: The common thread is probably high agency—someone who clearly takes matters into their own hands and delivers great results. Someone who wants to push something new into the product or start a new initiative.

Second is being creative-obsessed or customer-obsessed. You can tell when someone really cares. Whether they’re on the GTM side making sure customers understand how to use the tool and are cared for, or on the engineering side making sure every interaction is executed perfectly so creatives don’t waste their time with suboptimal interactions. Those are the two main things I look for.

Jon Lai: It sounds like it’s working, given all these fantastic folks who’ve joined recently. With the new funds from the Series A, what are you most excited to do? What does the next five years look like?

Weber Wong: Primarily building out the team, especially engineering—we’re hiring very aggressively there. Scaling up GTM in sales and marketing. Making sure we bring in high-quality folks and keep the magic we have culturally while keeping talent density very high. We may need to move offices soon because we’re growing.

Jon Lai: Wow, you’re moving offices again? That’s the third move right?

Weber Wong: Yeah. In our building in Williamsburg called Domino Sugar Refinery, they’re still building it out. As soon as they finish the floor above us, we get too big and move up to that floor. Then they start building the floor above us and we might need to move to that one. We’ve done that twice already, so it might be time to do it again.

Jon Lai: Flora is literally moving on up in life—physically too.

To close out with a prediction: if everything goes well and FLORA fulfills its potential, what does the creative industry look like in five years? How will things be different?

Weber Wong: The main change is that the creative means of production will have changed. It will be a lot easier to produce a lot of content and assets. That may restructure how creative teams are built and creative companies are constructed. In general, the value of rote manual creative labor will go down, but the value of taste will actually go up. If you have great taste, it suddenly becomes super easy to scale that.

Look at an agency—they get paid partially for rote manual labor, but also because they’re the best creatives in the world. I think value will accrue to those who are really great creatives, even though the initial narrative was that everyone can create now and the best creatives are going to lose their jobs. I have a fairly contrarian view based on the fact that we work with all these really great folks. When we put FLORA in the hands of the best creatives in the world, they make the best work we’ve ever seen. I expect they’ll continue to do very well.

Jon Lai: We’ve heard from professional storytellers in Hollywood and gaming that AI isn’t replacing the best storytellers—it’s extending their reach, expanding what’s possible, arming them with tools to do more with the time they have. For the best creatives, the main bottleneck is time—how much time they have in a day, how many years are left in their career. Wouldn’t the world be a richer place if you could get another ten years of output from the best movie directors, game designers, novelists, and storytellers?

Weber Wong: Definitely.
